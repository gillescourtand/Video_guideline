1. Key Concepts in Digital Video

A digital video file is a sequence of still images (frames) captured at regular time intervals. Each frame is a rectangular grid of pixels — the smallest sampling unit. Every pixel has a numerical value encoding its brightness (and color for color sensors). Understanding four concepts is essential before choosing hardware:

Spatial resolution
How many pixels cover your scene. Higher = more detail. But it costs storage and computation.
Temporal resolution
How many frames per second (fps). Higher = smoother capture of fast movement, but larger files.
Dynamic range
The ratio between the brightest and darkest thing the sensor can distinguish simultaneously. Low dynamic range = washed-out highlights or black shadows.
Image contrast
The difference in brightness between the animal and its background. The single most important factor for automated detection.
Figure 1 — From physical scene to stored video file. The analog-to-digital converter (ADC) converts photosite charge into integer pixel values; the codec compresses those values for storage.

Calculating the Resolution You Need

Before buying a camera, estimate the minimum sensor resolution for your experimental setup. The logic follows Nyquist's sampling theorem: sample at least twice the spatial frequency you want to resolve.

pixels_needed = (arena_width_mm / required_precision_mm) × 2

Example: arena = 250 mm, precision = 1 mm/pixel
→ 250 × 2 = 500 pixels minimum along the widest dimension
Practical tip: For standard open-field or fear-conditioning boxes (25–30 cm wide), a 640 × 480 px sensor is generally sufficient for whole-body detection. You need higher resolution only if you want to track individual body parts or measure very small movements (< 1 mm).

3. Sensor Resolution & Optics

Sensor Size vs. Pixel Size Trade-off

More pixels is not always better. The key parameter for low-light behavioral recording is photosite size (the physical size of each light-collecting element on the sensor, expressed in µm). Larger photosites collect more photons per unit time, improving sensitivity and signal-to-noise ratio — at the cost of lower spatial resolution for the same sensor footprint.

Figure 3 — Same physical sensor area, different photosite density. For behavioral assays in dim infrared light, larger photosites usually outperform a higher-megapixel color sensor.
Key finding (FreezingPoint 102c): Comparative testing of a high-definition color camera (Basler, 1152 × 852 px, 50 fps) vs. a low-definition infrared camera (iMetronic, 300 × 280 px, 25 fps) showed ICC values of 0.815–0.817 vs. 0.927–0.938 respectively. The infrared camera achieved better agreement with human scoring despite four times fewer pixels. Digital downsampling of the HD video did not improve agreement (Wilcoxon p = 0.43). Illumination modality and image contrast were the primary drivers, not spatial resolution.

Choosing a Lens

The lens must be matched to your sensor format (physical size of the sensor in mm) and your working distance. Use this formula to estimate the required focal length:

focal_length = (sensor_width_mm × working_distance_mm) / field_of_view_mm

Example: sensor = 6.4 mm, distance = 400 mm, arena = 250 mm
→ f = (6.4 × 400) / 250 = 10.2 mm focal length
Manufacturer configurators: Basler offers a free lens selector tool that computes focal length, depth of field, and field of view from your experimental parameters. Use it — the math above is a quick estimate only.

Global vs. Rolling Shutter

Most scientific cameras offer a global shutter (all pixels exposed simultaneously) or a rolling shutter (rows exposed sequentially). For fast-moving animals, rolling shutter can introduce image distortion ("jello effect"). For slow behaviors like freezing, rolling shutter is generally acceptable.

Flickering: a Common but Fixable Problem

When recording under fluorescent or LED lights powered by the mains grid, you may observe periodic brightness oscillations in the video — invisible to the naked eye but disruptive to automated analysis. This happens because the light flickers at the mains frequency (50 Hz in Europe, 60 Hz in the US), and the camera frame rate is not synchronized to it.

Figure 5 — Flickering occurs when frame exposure does not integrate over a full mains cycle. Solutions: set exposure to a multiple of the mains period (20 ms for 50 Hz; 16.7 ms for 60 Hz), use DC-powered LEDs, or use infrared illumination on a stable driver.
Anti-flicker rules:
  • Europe (50 Hz): set exposure to a multiple of 20 ms (e.g., 20, 40, or 60 ms).
  • USA/Canada (60 Hz): set exposure to a multiple of 16.7 ms (approximately 17 ms).
  • Use LED drivers with PWM frequency above 1 kHz, or constant-current DC drivers.
  • Infrared LED panels with stable DC supply are completely flicker-free.

5. Lighting

Scene illumination is the parameter that most strongly determines detection quality — more than spatial resolution. Two aspects matter: intensity (enough photons for the sensor) and uniformity (consistent brightness across the arena).

Visible light (white / warm LED)
  • Standard, cheap, widely available
  • Can disturb or habituate animals
  • Good contrast if animal coat differs from floor
  • Affected by reflections and shadows
  • Requires careful uniformity management
Infrared (IR) illumination ~850 nm
  • Invisible to most rodents (> 700 nm)
  • Enables recording in complete darkness
  • Requires IR-sensitive (monochrome) camera
  • More uniform than visible panels in typical setups
  • Recommended for fear conditioning, anxiety tests

IR Filter Considerations

Color cameras include an infrared-cut filter on the sensor to prevent IR wavelengths from shifting color balance. This filter blocks IR illumination. Options:

  1. Use a monochrome camera — no IR filter by default, IR-sensitive out of the box.
  2. Remove the IR filter from a color camera (check if user-serviceable on your model; voids warranty on some).
  3. Use a dedicated IR camera (e.g., iMetronic, Basler ace with monochrome sensor).

Illumination Uniformity

Non-uniform lighting is one of the most common causes of false positives in automated detection — tracking software may mistake a bright or dark patch on the floor for an animal. Practical measures:

Figure 6 — Single overhead spot creates a gradient that can confound threshold-based detection. Use four or more diffuse sources positioned at arena corners, or a large-area LED panel with a diffusion sheet.
Uniformity checklist:
  • Record an empty arena and measure pixel intensity in at least 9 zones. The coefficient of variation (CV) should be < 10%.
  • Use matte, non-reflective floor material (avoid glass or polished plastic).
  • Consider a circular polarizing filter on the camera lens if specular reflections are unavoidable.
  • Check for cage-wall reflections when using white arenas with high-intensity illumination.

6. Connectivity & Data Throughput

The camera–computer interface limits maximum image size × frame rate. Calculate the raw data rate of your intended recording before choosing an interface:

raw_data_rate (MB/s) = width × height × bytes_per_pixel × fps / 1,000,000

Example: 1920 × 1080, 8-bit monochrome (1 byte/px), 50 fps
→ 1920 × 1080 × 1 × 50 / 1e6 = 103.7 MB/s → needs USB 3.0 or GigE

7. Compression & Codecs

Video compression is an often-overlooked but critical factor for automated behavioral analysis. Compression algorithms introduce two types of artifacts that can degrade tracking accuracy:

Spatial artifacts (within a frame)
Block boundaries, ringing near edges, loss of fine texture. Caused by aggressive quantization in the DCT step of codecs like H.264.
Temporal artifacts (between frames)
Ghosting, smearing, false movement in stationary regions. Caused by inaccurate motion estimation in P-frames and B-frames.
Figure 7 — GOP structure. I-frames are full images; P and B frames store only differences. More I-frames = larger files but better frame quality. For behavioral analysis, keep the GOP size small (every 1–5 s, i.e., GOP = fps × 1..5).

Common mistake: Recording with default H.264 settings from consumer cameras often uses a keyframe interval of 300 frames (10 s at 30 fps). Slow or subtle movements between keyframes can be badly corrupted by predictive coding. Always check and reduce the keyframe interval if using H.264 or H.265.

Container Formats

The container wraps the compressed video data with metadata. For scientific use, prefer containers with broad software support:

  • .avi — widely compatible; use with MJPEG or uncompressed. Avoid with H.265.
  • .mp4 — universal; works well with H.264/H.265. Use for sharing.
  • .mkv — lossless container; supports any codec; best for archiving.
  • .tiff sequence — one image per frame; lossless; ideal for image analysis pipelines (large storage requirement).
